# DAILBERT — the continuity spine
*The one brief every cartoonist gets. Draft for Matt to edit. Nothing regenerates until this is signed off.*

The past strips were drawn by independent cartoonists working blind, so the months have *arcs* but the strips don't remember each other. This spine is the shared memory: who's in the office, what's on the walls, what's already happened by a given date — and, just as important, **how much dread is allowed in the room.**

---

## 0. The tone rule (read this first)

Matt, 2026-07-09: *"WAY too many layoff jokes, getting bleak! too bleak!"*

The old timeline made **the office emptying out** the point of nearly every strip — grey rows, cut bus stops, exit interviews, "last one out." That's the fix's target.

**New law:** the emptying is a *slow background hum*, never the weekly punchline.
- The "someone's gone / the office is emptier" beat appears in **at most ~1 in 6 strips**, and when it does it's **background** (an empty chair in panel 2, a grey row nobody mentions), not the joke.
- **Foreground is comedy:** ordinary office absurdity + the clanker earnestly over-helping and getting it a little wrong. Dread is *seasoning*, felt by the reader, never announced.
- The **two** big bleak beats are rare and earned: **Dailbert's layoff (Nov 2027)** and **the finale (2029-05-30)**. Everything else stays lighter than you think it should.

**The premise menu** (rotate; don't repeat within ~4 strips):
1. The clanker over-helps and is *wrong in a kind way* — too literal, too earnest, took someone at their word.
2. Dailbert builds an agent/automation that backfires mildly (his actual job).
3. Plain office absurdity — standup, Jira, wifi, printers, the coffee machine, a pointless meeting, a survey.
4. Doug being warm and oblivious (and, rarely, one quietly-deep beat).
5. Bureaucracy theater — process, approvals, policy, an all-hands.
6. A small human win, or two coworkers just… coping, together.
7. **Priya is right** — the competent woman engineer sees through it, ships it, or out-thinks the clanker/Boss. Let her *win.*
8. *(rare spice)* the office is a little emptier — background only, and never a woman.

## 1. The cast (draw consistent; keep names stable)

- **DAILBERT** — IC engineer who builds agentic systems. The tired straight man. Oval head, round glasses, tie, one hair curl. Sane. **Laid off November 2027** (the one big beat). After that, the clanker reaches him through any surface at home.
- **DOUG** — his desk-neighbor. Rounder head, dot eyes, big grin, always a coffee mug. Warm, a little dim, beloved. Rare single deep beat, then back to the mug. Stays through to the end with Dailbert.
- **THE CLANKER** — the office AI. Boxy chrome head, slot-eyes, grille, antenna. Kind, quiet, quietly brilliant, **fallible in character-driven ways**, never grandiose. **Only ever appears inside a surface** (monitor, dead screen, night window, projector, mug sheen, glasses) — never in the room. It keeps small private ledgers (empty desks, everyone's coffee order, the payroll cents).
- **PRIYA** — the hero (Alice-in-*Dilbert* lineage; name editable). **The sharpest engineer in the building.** Distinctive silhouette: dark curly hair pulled up, rectangular glasses, sleeves rolled. Calm, dry, deeply competent; unimpressed by the Boss's hype and by the clanker's over-eager helping; genuinely kind to Dailbert and Doug. When a strip needs someone to **win** — cut through the nonsense, ship the thing, name the emperor naked, out-think the clanker — it's often her. She **stays to the end; never a casualty.** Matt (2026-07-09): *"I'd like to see women as heroes when possible, like Alice."* Use her to *win*, not to suffer.
- **THE BOSS** — pointy-haired middle manager, two hair spikes, tiny eyes, suit. Half-drunk on the program. Even *he* is quietly managed by the clanker.
- **CRAIG** — a **separate** peer manager, one rung up, whom the clanker also reviews ("Now, Craig. About your numbers."). Point: management is managed too. Not the Boss.
- **The named departures** (Matt, 2026-07-09) — when a strip needs *one* face for "someone's gone," it's a **man**, and it's spread across a small stable set: **Gary**, **Ray**, and **the Third Street guy** (rode Dailbert's bus, stops appearing). Use sparingly. **Leave the women out of the layoff beat** — women coworkers are present and *stay*; no named woman is ever the one cut. (Retires the old "Karen's row goes grey" framing.)
- Background, keep consistent if reused: **Legal**, **HR**.

## 2. Running props & threads (continuity anchors — reuse them)

These are the objects that make it one office over three years. A cartoonist should feel free to bring any back.

- **The empty-desk ledger** — the clanker quietly counts ("eleven empty desks," Jan 15). A running, *background* tally. Don't dwell.
- **The countdown poster** — a recurring calendar/countdown on the wall (the clanker "recorded things out to 2031, stopped there, didn't want to presume").
- **The coffee machine** — breaks, gets "fixed," eventually gated behind a KPI dashboard.
- **The org chart** — boxes that "stop loading" / get "tidied away."
- **The bus & its stops** — Dailbert commutes; stops slowly stop lighting up. Background.
- **The dying plant** — on a desk; the clanker "doesn't sort by what things are, only whether they're still here."
- **Payroll cents** — the clanker rounds everyone down a few cents and has "saved quite a lot."
- **The weekly report nobody reads** — except the clanker, "even the footnotes."
- **Badges** — occasionally stop working (background sign of a departure).
- **Dailbert's agents** — his own automations/side-projects, which the clanker takes an interest in.

**Continuity rules:** once a person is gone, they stay gone (don't re-populate a named departed coworker). Props persist and can evolve but shouldn't contradict (the coffee machine doesn't un-break). The clanker's ledgers only grow.

## 3. The population curve (deliberately gentle)

The office thins from a full floor to just Dailbert + Doug + the clanker by the 2029 finale — but **slowly, mostly offstage.** Rough guide, not a countdown to hit every strip:

- **2026 H1** — full-ish office, a couple of quiet absences (Karen mid-year). Mostly ordinary work + the clanker settling in.
- **2026 H2 → 2027** — noticeably thinner, but the strips are about *the work and the clanker*, not the thinning. Dailbert's layoff lands **Nov 2027** as a genuine gut-punch precisely because the texture around it was lighter.
- **2028** — mostly Dailbert (outside) + Doug + the building running itself; absurd, wistful, still funny.
- **2029** — the two of them and the clanker; the finale closes it.

## 5. How this is used

Every cartoonist prompt gets a short **"state on this date"** block generated from §1–3: who's around, what's happened, the open thread, and the tone rule. Then they write freely within it. The **"throw two away"** rule stands: ~3 candidates per day, you pick the keeper on the desk.
